About the Game

Ghost of Tsushima is Sucker Punch Productions' open-world samurai epic set in 1274 during the first Mongol invasion of Japan. Jin Sakai, the last of his clan, must abandon the samurai code to wage guerrilla war against the invaders as the legendary Ghost. The walkthrough captures the full campaign across two parts on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling, no commentary, including the Iki Island expansion content.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. The Mongol Invasion

    The campaign opens on Komoda Beach in the autumn of 1274, with the entire fighting strength of Tsushima Island — eighty samurai retainers under Lord Shimura — assembled to repel a Mongol Empire amphibious assault. Jin Sakai, Shimura's nephew and adopted son, rides at the head of the formation. The chapter's first hour is the franchise's most cinematic and devastating sequence: a formal samurai charge across an open beach, met by Mongol horse archers, war machines, and the Khan's elite vanguard. The Tsushima samurai are systematically annihilated.

    The chapter's central event is Jin's defeat at the hands of Khotun Khan, the Mongol commander, who reveals during the duel that he has studied the samurai code precisely so that he can exploit its rigidity. Jin is wounded, left for dead, and Shimura is captured. The chapter cuts to black on a beach littered with the bodies of every man Jin has known since childhood.

    The chapter's second half opens with Jin's recovery, his rescue by the smuggler Yuna, and the slow restoration of his combat capability across rural southern Tsushima. The campaign's central question is articulated by Yuna in a quiet riverside scene: can a samurai who has lost his entire order still call himself a samurai? The chapter ends with Jin riding toward Castle Kaneda — the fortress where Shimura is held — committed to a rescue mission that requires methods the samurai code explicitly forbids.

  2. 2

    2. The Tale of Sensei Ishikawa

    The campaign's first major ally tale and one of its most thematically complete arcs. Sensei Ishikawa, the legendary master of the Sakai Bow, has spent the post-invasion months hunting his fallen student Tomoe, who has defected to the Mongol forces. The chapter is structured as a hunting partnership across northern Izuhara, with Ishikawa teaching Jin the master-level bow techniques that will define the rest of the campaign's ranged combat.

    The chapter's emotional centerpiece is the slow revelation of Ishikawa's relationship to Tomoe. The story is not simple betrayal — Tomoe was a child Ishikawa raised from a sold daughter, trained as both archer and survivor, and her defection is rooted in disagreements about the samurai code that the chapter dwells on at length. Ishikawa's eventual confrontation with her, staged in a windswept rice field at dusk, refuses easy moral closure.

    The chapter ends with Tomoe either dead or escaped — the player's choice — and Ishikawa permanently joining Jin's growing alliance. The bow upgrades unlocked in this chapter become essential for the campaign's late combat scenarios.

  3. 3

    3. The Tale of Lady Masako

    The campaign's vengeance arc. Lady Masako Adachi, the matriarch of the Adachi samurai clan, is the sole survivor of a coordinated murder of her entire family by an unknown faction during the invasion's chaos. The chapter is structured as a series of investigations across central Tsushima, with Masako tracking down the conspirators one by one through a combination of stealth infiltrations and dueling encounters.

    The chapter's mechanical focus is the Heavenly Strike, a single-hit-kill technique Masako teaches Jin in exchange for his help. The technique unlocks the campaign's most spectacle-heavy assassination sequences, with chained killings across enemy encampments. The chapter also dwells on Masako's relationship to her grown children — most dead, some still missing, and one whose role in the murders remains uncertain throughout the chapter.

    The chapter's climax is the confrontation with the conspiracy's true architect, whose identity recontextualizes the entire arc. Masako's vengeance is granted, but the chapter ends in moral ambiguity — Masako is now a woman with no family and no purpose, permanently joining Jin's alliance but visibly hollowed by what the campaign has put her through.

  4. 4

    4. The Liberation of Castle Kaneda

    The campaign's first major military set-piece and Act 1's climactic chapter. Jin, now leading a growing army of allies — Yuna, Ishikawa, Masako, the rōnin Ryuzo, and Jin's friend Taka — launches a coordinated assault on Castle Kaneda, where Lord Shimura is being held by Mongol forces under the lieutenant Temuge. The chapter is structured as a multi-front assault, with infiltration sequences, sustained combat against the castle garrison, and the campaign's first major sword duel against a named Mongol commander.

    The chapter's central reveal is the mass execution of Tsushima farmers and rōnin used by the Mongols as living shields during the castle's defense. Jin's discovery of the bodies — staged in a sustained walk through the castle's lower courtyard — provides the chapter's emotional pivot. The samurai code prohibits the methods that could have prevented this. The Ghost path, increasingly, looks like the only one that works.

    The chapter ends with Lord Shimura's rescue, Temuge's death at Jin's hands using a non-samurai technique, and the campaign's first sustained ideological conflict between Jin and Shimura. The act ends with Jin's growing identity as the Ghost beginning to fracture the most important relationship in his life.

  5. 5

    5. The Black Hand

    The campaign's mid-second-act assassination arc. Mongol intelligence has identified Jin as the Ghost of Tsushima, and Khotun Khan dispatches the Black Hand — a squad of Mongol assassins specifically trained to neutralize ninja-style insurgents — to hunt him. The chapter is structured as a reversed pursuit, with Jin laying ambushes for the assassins across Toyotama's coastal forests.

    The chapter's combat highlights are some of the campaign's hardest duels. The Black Hand fighters are mechanically distinct from regular Mongol troops — faster, more aggressive, and equipped with poisoned blades that the parry system must learn to read. The chapter introduces the Ghost stance, the most powerful of Jin's four sword stances, in a ritualistic late-night sequence that dwells on the cost of becoming the man the campaign is asking him to be.

    The chapter ends with the Black Hand eliminated, Jin's reputation among the Mongols transformed from inconvenience to existential threat, and Khotun Khan personally committing to ending the Ghost.

  6. 6

    6. The Tale of Yuna's Brother

    One of the campaign's most emotionally weighted arcs. Taka, Yuna's younger brother and one of Jin's closest friends, has been a steady presence since the chapter following the Komoda Beach defeat. The chapter is structured as a series of small village-level events — Taka's growing confidence as a blacksmith, his quiet courage during ambushes, his role in maintaining the alliance's logistical chain — that establish him as central to the campaign's emotional fabric.

    The chapter's mid-point is Taka's capture during a Mongol raid on a coastal village. The rescue mission is staged across a stormy beach assault and a final confrontation with the Mongol commander Bartu. The chapter's pacing is deliberately deceptive — every system in the game trains the player to expect successful hostage rescues, and the chapter's final beat refuses that.

    Taka is executed in front of Jin, in a slow-motion sequence that the campaign refuses to soften. The execution becomes the campaign's pivotal moment for Yuna, for Jin's relationship with Shimura, and for the audience's understanding of what the Mongol occupation actually costs. The chapter ends with Yuna's grief reshaping the alliance's purpose.

  7. 7

    7. The Defense of Castle Shimura

    The campaign's largest military engagement. Lord Shimura's home fortress comes under coordinated Mongol assault, and Jin — now openly operating as the Ghost — leads the defense alongside his uncle. The chapter is structured as a multi-phase castle siege, with archer-line defenses, courtyard combat against waves of Mongol troops, and a final dueling sequence on the castle's upper terraces.

    The chapter's central tension is the open conflict between Jin and Shimura's methods. Shimura insists on samurai-code defense; Jin uses smoke bombs, poison-tipped kunai, and assassinations to give the defenders the tactical advantage that the code would forbid. The chapter dwells on the campaign's central theme — that the code is honorable but insufficient, and that honoring it would have ended the campaign in massacre.

    The chapter ends with the castle held, the Mongol assault repulsed, and Lord Shimura formally requesting that the Shogun arrest his own nephew for dishonorable conduct. The campaign's third-act tragedy is now committed.

  8. 8

    8. Kamiagata and the Final Liberation

    The campaign's third-act regional liberation. Jin moves north into Kamiagata, Tsushima's snow-covered northernmost province, in pursuit of the final stronghold of Khotun Khan's army. The biome is rendered as the campaign's most visually stark — slate-gray skies, snow-fall combat, and the bleached aesthetic of an occupation winter.

    The chapter's mid-section is the recovery and integration of every ally the campaign has built across Acts 1 and 2 — Ishikawa, Masako, Norio, Yuna — into a unified attack on the Khan's command. The combat encounters scale to match, with multi-ally ambush sequences and coordinated stealth assaults that demand mastery of every system the campaign has taught.

    The chapter ends with the Liberation of Iki subregion via the now-cleared Kamiagata north coast, Khotun Khan's army reduced to a single command post, and the final assault now staged.

  9. 9

    9. The Final Duel

    The campaign's climactic confrontation. Jin pursues Khotun Khan to the campaign's geographical apex, a ruined coastal shrine where the Khan has retreated with his last loyal forces. The chapter is structured as a sustained final-mission sequence: a beach assault, a sustained combat through ruined temples, and the climactic one-on-one duel.

    The duel with Khotun Khan is the campaign's mechanical apex. The Khan uses every sword stance the campaign has taught Jin, and the fight demands the full toolkit — stance switching, perfect parries, Ghost-stance opening attacks, and the duel-finishing strikes the campaign has carefully unlocked across all four acts. The fight is staged in a single arena, in real-time, with no cuts and no resurrection.

    The chapter ends with the Khan's death by Jin's blade, the Tsushima invasion ended, and the campaign's true final crisis introduced in the closing minutes: Lord Shimura's arrival with imperial soldiers, with a warrant for Jin's arrest.

  10. 10

    10. The Choice

    The campaign's defining moral chapter. Lord Shimura — Jin's uncle, his adoptive father, and the man whose entire moral system Jin has just exceeded in order to save Tsushima — meets him in a flowering field outside the campaign's final temple. The chapter is structured as a single conversation, a single duel, and a single irrevocable decision.

    The duel itself is staged with the campaign's most decorated cinematography and one of its most acclaimed musical cues. Shimura is genuinely trying to kill Jin; Jin is genuinely trying to defeat without killing him. The duel ends with Shimura defeated and disarmed, and the campaign's final choice handed to the player: honor your uncle's request and kill him, granting him an honorable death by family blade, or spare him, refusing the samurai code's final demand and accepting permanent exile from the order.

    The chapter ends with either ending played out in full, and the campaign's central question — what does it cost to save the world by abandoning the codes you were raised in? — answered by the player's choice. Either ending is canonical. Neither is comfortable.

  11. 11

    11. Iki Island (DLC)

    The Director's Cut's expansion campaign, set on the smaller island of Iki off Tsushima's western coast. Jin travels to Iki in pursuit of rumors that a new Mongol force, led by a Khan called Ankhsar Khatun, has subjugated the island in the months since the main campaign's events. The chapter's opening sequence is structured as a deliberate retread of Komoda Beach — a samurai landing under fire — except this time, the failure is Jin's alone.

    The chapter dwells on Jin's family history. Iki is the island where Kazumasa Sakai, Jin's father, died in battle when Jin was a child. The Eagle Tribe and the Sakai clan have a centuries-old blood feud, and Jin's arrival reopens wounds the locals have nursed for generations. The chapter weaves the Eagle's shamanic practices — drug-induced visions, animal-spirit rituals — into Jin's grief in ways the main campaign refuses to attempt.

    The chapter's combat highlights include the mounted Mongol cavalry, a faster and more aggressive enemy class than anything in the main campaign, and the introduction of animal companions as side-quest rewards.

  12. 12

    12. Ankhsar Khatun

    The DLC's climactic confrontation. The Eagle is revealed as a female warlord whose shamanic practices have given her army the most unconventional combat repertoire in the franchise. The final dueling sequence with Ankhsar Khatun is staged in a hallucinatory ritual chamber, with Jin's vision distorted by Eagle Tribe drugs and the Khan herself appearing as multiple simultaneous projections.

    The chapter ends with Ankhsar Khatun's defeat, the Iki population freed from Mongol control, and the campaign's final scene: Jin returning to Tsushima with his understanding of his father, of the Sakai legacy, and of what it means to be a son who has surpassed every model he was raised against. The DLC closes the franchise's first chapter with one of its most thematically satisfying resolutions.
