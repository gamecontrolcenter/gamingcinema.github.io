About the Game

Silent Hill 2 Remake is Bloober Team and Konami's full ground-up remake of the 2001 PlayStation 2 psychological horror classic. James Sunderland returns to the cursed town of Silent Hill after receiving a letter from his late wife Mary. The walkthrough captures the full campaign across two parts on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling, no commentary. The cinematic playthrough preserves the slow, dread-filled pacing of the original.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. The Letter — Arrival in Silent Hill

    The campaign opens with James Sunderland parked at a roadside lookout overlooking the foggy lakeside town of Silent Hill, re-reading a letter from his wife Mary — who has been dead for three years. The letter promises she is waiting for him in "their special place" in Silent Hill, a town the couple visited on their honeymoon. The opening sequence is staged in a public restroom, with James studying himself in a cracked mirror as the camera holds on his face for an uncomfortably long take. The remake establishes its psychological tone before introducing a single monster.

    The chapter's exploration loop is the foggy forest path down into the town proper. James descends through overgrown trails, abandoned tourist signage, and the first scattered evidence of the town's degradation — a barbed-wire-blocked road, a half-buried memorial. The fog system, rendered with the remake's new volumetric lighting, is one of the most acclaimed atmospheric achievements of the project. Combat is not yet introduced; James is alone with the fog.

    The chapter ends with James reaching the edge of the town, finding it abandoned, and discovering the corpse of an unknown woman in an alley. The body is unsettling not because it is grotesque but because it is so visibly recent. The first Lying Figure — the limbless, vomiting humanoid that will become the campaign's signature enemy — emerges from the fog moments later. The campaign's combat tutorial is silent, brutal, and immediate.

  2. 2

    2. Eastside Silent Hill

    The campaign's introductory open-zone chapter. James explores the eastern half of Silent Hill — Lindsey Street, Saul Street, Vachss Road — through abandoned cars, half-flooded sidewalks, and the recurring radio static that signals nearby creatures. The chapter's exploration loop introduces the remake's reworked combat fundamentals: melee primary engagement with the wooden plank and lead pipe, ammunition scarcity that forces every shotgun shell to count, and the new dodge mechanic that the original game lacked.

    The chapter's central encounter is the first meeting with Angela Orosco in the Rosewater Park cemetery. Angela — a young woman who has come to Silent Hill searching for her own mother — is introduced through a sustained dialogue scene that the remake stages with significantly more empathy than the original. Her trauma is visible. Her relationship to the town is established as fundamentally different from James's. She gives him a knife that becomes a recurring narrative motif.

    The chapter ends with James reaching the Wood Side Apartments, the campaign's first major interior dungeon, and the radio's static building to a full-volume scream as he steps inside.

  3. 3

    3. The Wood Side Apartments

    The remake's first sustained dungeon and one of its most acclaimed environmental designs. The Wood Side Apartments are rendered as a multi-floor residential building where every door tells a story — partially preserved family lives, scattered mail, photographs that suggest the residents disappeared mid-routine rather than fled. The chapter's exploration loop weaves combat against the apartment's three resident enemy types (Lying Figures, Mannequins, the first Bubble Head Nurse encounter) with sustained puzzle-solving — the clock puzzle, the doll puzzle, and the light bulb puzzle are reworked into significantly more interactive forms than the original.

    The chapter introduces Eddie Dombrowski in the basement, gnawing on raw food beside an old freezer that the chapter explicitly does not let the player open. Eddie's introduction is staged with deliberate ambiguity — he is socially awkward, panicked, and quietly inscrutable. The chapter dwells on James's response to him: not curiosity, but a kind of unease that the campaign will pay off only much later.

    The chapter ends with the first Pyramid Head encounter — a sustained sequence behind iron bars where James watches the towering figure stand silently and observe him before walking away. The remake stages the encounter with no combat, no music, and a long take that lands as one of the most acclaimed introductions in modern survival horror.

  4. 4

    4. Blue Creek Apartments

    A companion building to the Wood Side complex, accessible via a connecting passageway. The exploration loop is structurally similar but tonally darker — Blue Creek is rendered as significantly more decayed, with collapsed floors, flooded corridors, and several enemy classes the previous chapter did not stage. The remake introduces the Abstract Daddy mini-boss in the apartment's furnace room, with a fight design that demands stealth-and-strike pacing rather than the open combat the Wood Side fights permitted.

    The chapter's central narrative event is Angela's second appearance — found in a trance state in an apartment that the remake renders with explicit visual parallels to her family home. Her dialogue here is significantly more pointed than the original, with the campaign laying foundation for the chapter where her own history will be revealed.

    The chapter ends with James finally locating the apartment number Mary's letter mentioned — and finding it empty, mocking, with a single tape recording left behind.

  5. 5

    5. Brookhaven Hospital

    The campaign's most extensive single dungeon. Brookhaven Hospital is rendered as a three-floor abandoned psychiatric facility, with examination rooms, patient wings, a morgue, and a roof access. The chapter's exploration loop is one of the most acclaimed in the franchise — the rooms are individually puzzles, the patient files are individually horrifying, and the Bubble Head Nurse enemy class is staged in the highest density the campaign deploys it.

    The chapter's central character introduction is Maria — a woman who is identical to Mary in appearance but radically different in personality. Maria's introduction is staged in the hospital lobby with deliberate seductive framing that James responds to in ways that the campaign refuses to soften. The chapter establishes the campaign's central question: what is Maria, what does she represent, and what is James actually doing in this town?

    The chapter ends with the shift to the Otherworld Hospital — the campaign's first sustained alternate-reality sequence, rendered in rust, blood, and grinding industrial sound. The Otherworld pivot is the campaign's mid-game environmental tone shift.

  6. 6

    6. The Historical Society

    A medium-length chapter that pivots the campaign's geography away from interior dungeons and into the town's historical record. James and Maria descend into the Silent Hill Historical Society, where the exploration loop weaves displays about the town's pre-twentieth-century history — its founding as a Native American sacred site, its colonial-era prison, its 1800s execution grounds — into the chapter's broader thematic argument about layered guilt.

    The chapter's central reveal is that the town's "special place" mentioned in Mary's letter has multiple possible meanings. The Historical Society reveals a network of underground tunnels connecting Silent Hill's hotel, prison, and lakefront. James's destination is now the Lakeview Hotel — the place he and Mary actually honeymooned — but the path requires descending into the town's historical foundation first.

    The chapter ends with the Society sealed shut behind James and Maria, the descent into the Toluca-era town below now committed.

  7. 7

    7. The Toluca Prison

    The campaign's most explicitly horror-themed sustained sequence. The Toluca Prison is a nineteenth-century execution facility buried beneath modern Silent Hill, rendered with full Otherworld aesthetics — rust, blood, mechanical fan sounds, and execution cells the campaign visits with deliberate slowness. The chapter weaves combat against Mannequins and a new enemy class — Creepers — through a layered architectural puzzle that demands careful map reading.

    The chapter's central event is the Maria sequence. Maria, separated from James earlier, is found in a cell within the prison's deepest level — and is killed in front of James by Pyramid Head, in a sustained sequence that the remake stages with brutal patience. The killing is the campaign's first explicit confrontation with Maria's role in James's psychology.

    The chapter ends with James continuing into the Labyrinth below the prison, alone, with the campaign's central question about Maria temporarily — and only temporarily — answered.

  8. 8

    8. The Labyrinth

    The campaign's most architecturally ambitious dungeon. The Labyrinth beneath Silent Hill is rendered as a multi-tiered maze of stone corridors, vertical drops, and impossibly nested chambers. The chapter's exploration loop demands the most careful map-reading of any sequence in the campaign, with no shortcuts and no signposting beyond the campaign's careful environmental detail.

    The chapter's central boss confrontation is the Eddie Dombrowski fight in the meat locker. Eddie — last seen in the Wood Side Apartments basement — has been operating in the same dimensional space as James and has fully snapped. His backstory, laid out across the chapter's audio logs and discovered photographs, frames him as a man who killed a high school bully and has been escalating since. The fight ends with Eddie dead at James's hand, James now visibly aware that he has killed a person, and the chapter's tonal weight permanently shifted.

    The chapter ends with James emerging from the Labyrinth onto the shore of Toluca Lake, with the Lakeview Hotel visible across the water.

  9. 9

    9. The Lakeview Hotel

    The campaign's climactic dungeon. The Lakeview Hotel is rendered as a half-burned, half-flooded resort that James and Mary visited on their honeymoon three years before the campaign's opening. The chapter's exploration loop weaves combat against the campaign's hardest enemy variants with sustained puzzle-solving across the hotel's restaurant, kitchen, lobby, and guest wings. The remake stages several rooms with explicit memory-overlay sequences where the present-day decay is intercut with flashes of the honeymoon visit.

    The chapter's central reveal is the videotape. James finds a tape from the original honeymoon, plays it on a still-functional VCR in the hotel's manager office, and the campaign's central truth lands in full: James killed Mary himself, three years ago, by smothering her with a pillow in her hospice bed. The letter that brought him to Silent Hill never existed. The entire campaign has been the town's response to a man who has been lying to himself about what he did.

    The chapter ends with James walking out of the manager's office and into the campaign's final sequence.

  10. 10

    10. The Final Confrontations

    The campaign's climactic chapter weaves multiple boss confrontations and the ending sequence into a single sustained finale. James fights through the hotel's collapsed upper floors, confronts the campaign's most aggressive Pyramid Head encounter (now staged as a direct combat fight rather than the previous chapters' avoidance scenarios), and ultimately reaches the hotel's roof for the campaign's final boss.

    The final boss fight is determined by the player's behavior across the entire campaign — how often James has examined Mary's letter, how he has responded to Maria, how he has treated his own health. The remake supports eight distinct endings: Leave (the most common, James departs Silent Hill), In Water (James joins Mary in the lake), Maria (James leaves with Maria), Rebirth (James seeks resurrection ritual), and several joke and bonus endings.

    The chapter ends with the campaign's chosen ending played out in full, and the credits rolling over a sustained shot of the foggy lake. The campaign's central question — whether James will ever leave Silent Hill, in any meaningful sense — is answered by whichever ending the player has earned.
