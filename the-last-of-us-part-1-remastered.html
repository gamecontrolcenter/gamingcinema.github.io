About the Game

The Last of Us Part I Remastered is Naughty Dog's PS5 ground-up remake of the 2013 PlayStation classic, with rebuilt assets, modern lighting, and updated AI. Smuggler Joel escorts immune teenager Ellie across a fungus-infested post-pandemic America in search of the Fireflies' research lab. The walkthrough captures the full main campaign across two parts on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling, no commentary, presented as a continuous cinematic playthrough.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. Hometown

    The campaign's prologue and one of the most influential opening hours in modern gaming. The chapter is staged in Austin, Texas, in 2013, on the night that a fungal pandemic — the Cordyceps Brain Infection — pushes the United States past the point of recovery. The player begins as Sarah, Joel's twelve-year-old daughter, exploring the family home as news reports of unrelated violence flicker on the TV. The pacing is deliberately domestic and ordinary, with Sarah's bedroom, her father's birthday gift to her, and the family routine establishing the emotional stakes the rest of the chapter will detonate.

    The chapter's central sequence is the escape from Austin. Joel returns from work in the middle of the night, the family — Joel, Sarah, and Joel's brother Tommy — attempts to flee the city by truck, and the player experiences the collapse of American civil order from the passenger seat. The sequence weaves road accidents, military checkpoints, infected pursuit through residential neighborhoods, and a flipped truck in a moment that has become canonical.

    The chapter's final scene is the death of Sarah. Shot by a panicked soldier under orders to neutralize anyone potentially infected, Sarah dies in Joel's arms on a darkened roadside. The scene is staged with no score, in long takes, and Troy Baker's performance — alongside Hana Hayes as Sarah — defines the entire campaign's emotional foundation. The chapter ends with a cut to black and the title card.

  2. 2

    2. The Quarantine Zone

    The chapter jumps forward twenty years. Joel is now a forty-eight-year-old smuggler operating out of the Boston Quarantine Zone, one of the last surviving U.S. military-controlled survivor enclaves. The chapter's first hour is structured as a tour of post-collapse America: ration lines, public executions, military checkpoints, and the deeply unhealthy ecosystem of black-market trade that keeps the QZ functioning. Joel's partnership with Tess, a fellow smuggler and his closest human connection, is established as both professional and quietly romantic.

    The chapter's central sequence is the botched smuggling job that brings Joel and Tess to the warehouse where Marlene, leader of the Fireflies resistance, presents them with their next contract: smuggle a fourteen-year-old girl named Ellie out of the QZ in exchange for a major cache of supplies. Ellie's introduction is staged with deliberate ambiguity — Joel does not yet know she is immune to the infection, and the negotiation that brings her into his care is purely transactional.

    The chapter closes with the trio sneaking out of the QZ at night, attempting to clear the city's Federal Disaster Response perimeter. The escape goes badly. A military scanner detects Ellie's bite scars, the patrol opens fire, and Joel kills a soldier in self-defense. The trio escapes into the post-apocalyptic outskirts of Boston with no clear path back. The chapter ends with the campaign committed to a journey nobody yet understands the scope of.

  3. 3

    3. The Outskirts

    The chapter dwells in the ruined outer boroughs of Boston, with Joel, Tess, and Ellie navigating the city's collapsed infrastructure toward a Firefly rendezvous point at the State House. The chapter introduces the campaign's combat fundamentals in full: stealth-first encounter design, the listen-mode echolocation system, the crafting bench, the molotov and shiv recipes, and the resource-scarcity logic that defines the campaign's tension.

    The chapter's central reveal is Ellie's immunity. Tess, scratched during a clicker encounter in a museum sequence, confronts Ellie about her bite — and discovers that Ellie was bitten three weeks ago and has shown no symptoms. Tess realizes she will turn within hours, but Ellie will not. The realization reframes the entire smuggling job. Tess insists Joel honor the contract and deliver Ellie to the Fireflies. The implications — that Ellie's blood could be the key to a vaccine — are deliberately understated.

    The chapter ends at the State House. Tess, now visibly turning, holds off a pursuing military patrol while Joel and Ellie escape through a back stairwell. Tess's death — staged in a sustained gunfight with Joel and Ellie watching from cover — is the campaign's first major loss after the prologue. The chapter ends with the two of them committed to a journey neither of them wants.

  4. 4

    4. Bill's Town

    A character-focused chapter set in Lincoln, Massachusetts, the trap-laden domain of Bill — a paranoid, conspiracy-driven survivor who owes Joel a favor and has the resources to provide a working vehicle. The chapter is staged across Lincoln's overgrown streets, with Bill's elaborate booby-trap network catching infected, military patrols, and Joel himself in sequence. The opening combat tutorial weaves stealth, environmental kills, and the campaign's first major clicker encounters into the chapter's geography.

    The chapter's central thread is Bill's quiet grief. Frank — Bill's partner, never named directly in the original cut, but present in his belongings and his suicide note — has died before Joel's arrival. The chapter's emotional centerpiece is the discovery of Frank's body in a safehouse attic, hanged, with a note explaining that he could not live with Bill's bunker-paranoia and the world it was preparing for. Bill reads the note in silence. The campaign's later expansions would revisit this story; the original cut leaves it intact and devastating.

    The chapter closes with Bill providing a working car battery, Joel and Ellie driving out of Lincoln in a salvaged Chevy pickup, and the journey moving toward Pittsburgh.

  5. 5

    5. Pittsburgh

    The campaign's most extended combat chapter. The driving sequence ends in an ambush — Joel and Ellie are run off the road by a Hunter blockade, and the rest of the chapter is structured around their escape on foot through the bombed-out downtown of Pittsburgh. The Hunters, a faction of post-collapse human survivors who prey on travelers, are introduced as the campaign's first sustained human enemy. The combat shifts from infected-focused stealth to human-on-human gunfights with significantly different mechanical rhythms.

    The chapter's open-world feel — multiple parallel routes, optional building infiltrations, and resource-rich exploration paths — gives it the campaign's most pure "survival horror open level" design. The hotel basement sequence, in particular, is one of the campaign's most acclaimed set-pieces: a flooded service tunnel, a generator restart, and a clicker swarm in pitch darkness that introduces the campaign's hardest stealth encounter to date.

    The chapter ends with Joel and Ellie meeting Henry and his younger brother Sam, fellow survivors of the Pittsburgh Hunters who are trying to reach a remote refuge. The four of them agree to travel together. The chapter closes with the group hidden in a Hunter-cleared apartment building, planning their escape across the Allegheny River.

  6. 6

    6. The Suburbs

    A shorter chapter that dwells in the relative quiet of Pittsburgh's outer suburbs as the four travelers prepare to cross the river and head west. The combat density drops significantly; the chapter's focus is on the developing dynamic between Ellie and Sam, two teenagers from radically different post-collapse upbringings, and on Joel's growing parental discomfort with how attached Ellie is becoming to anyone.

    The chapter's central event is the death of Sam. Bitten during the previous chapter's river crossing and concealed by Henry, Sam turns in the night and attacks Ellie. The campaign forces the player to watch Henry shoot his own brother before turning the weapon on himself. The scene is staged in a single sustained take, with no music, and lands as one of the campaign's most devastating losses.

    The chapter closes with Joel and Ellie burying the brothers in the suburbs and continuing west alone. The road has narrowed to just the two of them again, and the campaign's central relationship has nowhere left to deflect into.

  7. 7

    7. Tommy's Dam

    A reprieve chapter set at the Jackson Hole hydroelectric dam, where Joel's brother Tommy and his wife Maria have built a working post-collapse community. The chapter opens with Joel and Ellie arriving at the dam in the late afternoon, Tommy's reunion with Joel staged across the chapter's first half. The community is presented as the campaign's first genuinely functional post-collapse society, with farming, electricity, schools, and a working defensive perimeter.

    The chapter's central conflict is Joel's attempt to hand off Ellie. Convinced he is not the right person to escort her the rest of the way, Joel asks Tommy to take her instead. Ellie overhears the conversation, runs from the dam, and the chapter's middle act is a horseback pursuit across the Wyoming wilderness to bring her back. The conversation that follows — Ellie confronting Joel about Sarah, about what he is afraid of, about why he keeps pushing her away — is one of the campaign's most pivotal scenes.

    The chapter ends with Joel changing his mind. He will take Ellie the rest of the way himself. They depart Jackson the next morning, riding south toward the Fireflies' lab at the University of Eastern Colorado.

  8. 8

    8. The University

    A medium-length chapter set in the abandoned campus of the University of Eastern Colorado, where the Fireflies are rumored to have established their primary research facility. The campus is rendered in the campaign's most autumnal lighting — fall foliage, low sun, empty quads — and the chapter's exploration loop weaves Joel and Ellie through dormitories, lecture halls, and a Fireflies-converted biology department.

    The chapter's central reveal is that the Fireflies have moved. The University facility is abandoned, with audio logs and lab notes revealing that the research team relocated months ago to a hospital in Salt Lake City. The trip has been, in one sense, for nothing. The deeper realization — that the Fireflies are clearly conducting active research on the infection, with hints that the methodology is not benign — is left for the audience to put together.

    The chapter ends with Joel and Ellie ambushed by a Hunter patrol in the campus library. The escape sequence is brutal: Joel is impaled on a piece of rebar during a fall from a second-floor balcony, the wound is severe, and Ellie is forced to drag him to relative safety while a winter storm closes in. The chapter cuts to black with Joel unconscious and Ellie alone.

  9. 9

    9. Lakeside Resort

    The campaign's most acclaimed perspective-shift chapter. Joel is comatose for most of the chapter, recovering from his rebar wound in an abandoned lakeside vacation house, while Ellie — now the player character — hunts in the surrounding winter wilderness to keep them alive. The chapter is staged in heavy snow, with the player's vision limited and the cold pressing against the survival mechanics in ways the campaign has not previously demanded.

    The chapter's central thread is David, a charismatic survivor leader who befriends Ellie during her hunting expeditions. David's group has been pursuing Joel and Ellie since Pittsburgh, and David's slow grooming of Ellie — staged with deliberate menace — reveals that his community is composed of cannibals who have been preying on travelers all winter. The chapter pivots from survival drama to horror in the second act.

    The chapter ends with one of the most acclaimed extended sequences in the franchise: Ellie's hatchet murder of David in a burning restaurant kitchen, after his attempted assault. The scene is staged in flickering fire light, with no music, and lands as Ellie's irreversible loss of childhood. Joel — recovered enough to walk — arrives moments too late. The chapter closes with him carrying her, exhausted and traumatized, back into the woods.

  10. 10

    10. Bus Depot

    A short transitional chapter set in the spring, with Joel and Ellie having traveled into the high country surrounding Salt Lake City. The chapter dwells in the quiet aftermath of the Lakeside Resort. Ellie is visibly closed off, Joel is visibly aware of it, and the two of them spend most of the chapter not speaking.

    The chapter's emotional centerpiece is a giraffe sequence inside an abandoned bus depot. The wandering giraffe — a deliberate visual contradiction to the post-apocalyptic world the campaign has built — gives Ellie a single moment of genuine wonder, and Joel's quiet recognition that he is going to have to give her up to the Fireflies anchors the campaign's final emotional turn.

    The chapter ends with the two of them sighting Salt Lake City in the distance and beginning their descent toward the Firefly hospital.

  11. 11

    11. The Firefly Lab

    The campaign's climax. Joel and Ellie are intercepted at the river outside Salt Lake City by a Firefly patrol, knocked unconscious, and transported to St. Mary's Hospital, the Fireflies' research facility. The chapter opens with Joel recovering in a hospital bed under Marlene's supervision, learning that the surgical procedure required to extract a vaccine from Ellie's brain will kill her — and that the Fireflies have already prepped her for surgery.

    The chapter pivots to one of the franchise's most morally loaded set-pieces. Joel — armed by a sympathetic Firefly nurse — fights his way through the hospital's surgical wing, killing dozens of Fireflies along the way, including unarmed surgeons and the surgeon currently in mid-procedure on Ellie. The combat is staged without the campaign's usual stealth options; the design forces direct, sustained violence in a way the campaign has resisted up to this point. The implication is intentional.

    The chapter ends with Joel carrying Ellie out of the operating room, killing Marlene in the parking garage to prevent her pursuit, and lying to Ellie about what happened — telling her the Fireflies "found dozens like her" and that "there are no vaccines coming." The lie is staged in the truck cab, in close-up, with Joel watching Ellie's reaction. The chapter cuts to black on the highway out of Salt Lake.

  12. 12

    12. Jackson

    The campaign's epilogue, set months later in the Jackson Hole community. Joel and Ellie have walked back to Tommy's settlement, and the chapter is staged as a quiet domestic afternoon — Tommy and Maria's home, the community's children, the working farm. The combat is gone. The journey is over.

    The chapter's central scene is Ellie's confrontation with Joel on a Jackson hillside. She asks him directly whether he has been telling her the truth about the Fireflies. Joel repeats the lie, this time with more weight, and asks her to believe him. Ellie's response — "Okay" — is one of the most studied single-word performances in the medium. The campaign does not resolve the question of whether Ellie believes him or whether she is simply choosing to accept the lie in order to preserve what they have built.

    The chapter ends with the two of them walking together back toward the Jackson gate. The credits roll over the campaign's signature score. Part II is years away, but the seed of its central conflict — Joel's lie and the cost of it — is already planted in the final frame.
