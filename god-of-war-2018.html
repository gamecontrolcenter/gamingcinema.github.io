About the Game

God of War (2018) reboots Santa Monica Studio's franchise with a one-take camera, Norse mythology setting, and an emphasis on father-son storytelling. Kratos and his young son Atreus journey across Midgard, Alfheim, and Helheim to scatter the ashes of Kratos's wife from the highest peak in the realms. The walkthrough captures the full main campaign on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling, no commentary, presented as one continuous cinematic playthrough.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. The Marked Trees

    The campaign opens with Kratos — the Greek god of war whose entire mythology the franchise's prior installments built — felling the marked tree his wife Faye instructed him to cut before her death. The opening sequence is structured as a quiet domestic ritual, with Kratos preparing Faye's funeral pyre in the snowy woods outside their cabin. Faye's body is wrapped in cloth. Her son, Atreus — a small boy Kratos has spent most of his life ignoring — observes from a distance.

    The chapter dwells on the franchise's complete tonal reinvention. Where the original Greek-era God of War games were defined by hyperviolent rage, the 2018 reboot opens with the campaign's central thesis: Kratos has spent years in hiding in the Norse wilds, attempting to build a family he is unequipped to maintain. Faye's dying wish — that her ashes be spread from the highest peak in the Nine Realms — is the chapter's central narrative obligation.

    The chapter's pivot is the arrival of the Stranger — a heavily tattooed, snowy-bearded man who appears at the cabin asking for Kratos by his former name. The Stranger does not know Atreus exists. He has come for Kratos personally, and the resulting brawl is one of the most acclaimed opening boss confrontations in modern gaming. The fight ranges across the Wildwoods, through the cabin's exterior, and ends in a sustained mountainside duel that Kratos cannot decisively win. The chapter ends with the Stranger departing, mysteriously revived from what should have been a killing blow, and Kratos's growing recognition that the Nine Realms have noticed him.

  2. 2

    2. Path to the Mountain

    The chapter that opens the campaign's open-zone exploration. Kratos and Atreus depart the cabin with Faye's ashes, beginning the journey to the highest peak in the Nine Realms. The chapter is structured as a sustained traversal sequence through the Wildwoods, the Witch's Cave, and the Lake of Nine — Midgard's central region and the campaign's primary hub area.

    The chapter introduces the campaign's signature mechanics in full. The Leviathan Axe — the over-the-shoulder camera, the recallable throw, the freeze-and-shatter combat loop — replaces the franchise's classic Blades of Chaos as Kratos's primary weapon. Atreus's bow becomes the campaign's secondary projectile system, with the boy issuing arrows on Kratos's command rather than fighting independently.

    The chapter introduces Brok, the blue-skinned dwarven blacksmith whose shop at Tyr's Temple serves as the campaign's central upgrade hub. The chapter ends with Kratos and Atreus reaching the foot of the mountain, the World Serpent Jörmungandr revealed for the first time in the Lake of Nine — a being so large that the lake itself is curved around his body — and the campaign's scale shifted into open mythological territory.

  3. 3

    3. A Realm Between Realms

    The campaign's first sustained dungeon. Kratos and Atreus descend through the Bifröst tower at Tyr's Temple, the Norse god's hidden inter-realm portal system. The chapter introduces the campaign's central traversal device — the realm-shift bridge — that connects the Lake of Nine to the broader Nine Realms structure. Each pillar of the bridge corresponds to a different realm: Alfheim, Helheim, Muspelheim, Niflheim, Jötunheim, and others.

    The chapter dwells on the campaign's Norse-mythology setup. The lore of the Nine Realms, the political dynamics between Aesir and Vanir, and the absence of the Jötnar (the giants, hunted to functional extinction by Odin) are introduced through Mimir-narration and environmental detail. The chapter introduces Sindri, Brok's clean-obsessed brother and the campaign's secondary blacksmith.

    The chapter ends with Kratos and Atreus's first inter-realm crossing committed, and the campaign's central question — whether Faye's dying wish requires the journey to involve all Nine Realms — fully posed.

  4. 4

    4. The Light of Alfheim

    The campaign's first sustained alternate-realm chapter. Kratos and Atreus travel to Alfheim, the bright-realm home of the Light and Dark Elves, in pursuit of the Light of Alfheim — a fragment of the realm's central energy source needed to power the inter-realm bridge. The chapter is structured as a sustained exploration of the realm's signature aesthetic: massive bright-elf temples, suspended platform networks, and the Light of Alfheim itself rendered as a vast, pulsing energy structure.

    The chapter dwells on the Alfheim civil war. The Light and Dark Elves are engaged in a perpetual conflict, with neither side rendered sympathetically. The chapter's combat highlights include the campaign's first sustained encounter with Dark Elves — fast, flying, projectile-heavy enemies that demand the player to integrate Atreus's archery into Kratos's combat rotation.

    The chapter ends with the Light of Alfheim acquired, Atreus suffering an unexpected and serious illness from the realm's pure-light energy, and the campaign's mid-act setup pivoting to the Witch of the Woods for medical aid.

  5. 5

    5. The Witch's Awakening

    The chapter that introduces the campaign's most important supporting character. The Witch of the Woods — a hooded forest healer Kratos and Atreus first encountered briefly in the early chapters — is revealed to be Freya, the former queen of the Vanir and one of Norse mythology's most powerful goddesses. The chapter is structured as a sustained character-revelation sequence at her woodland sanctuary.

    Freya's backstory is laid out across the chapter's dialogue: she was wife to Odin, exiled when the marriage failed, and stripped of her warrior abilities by Odin's curse. She has spent years hiding from Aesir attention and raising the wildlife of Midgard as her surrogate family. Her healing of Atreus brings the boy back from his illness, and her warning to Kratos — that violence is a contagion that will inevitably reach Atreus — becomes the campaign's central thematic conflict.

    The chapter ends with Atreus recovered, Freya revealed as a recurring ally, and the campaign's central conflict — whether Atreus will inherit Kratos's violence — fully active.

  6. 6

    6. The Magic Chisel

    A character-focused chapter that introduces the Stone Mason subquest. Kratos and Atreus search for the chisel that will allow them to open Tyr's Temple, the campaign's hub location. The chapter weaves combat with sustained environmental exploration, including the campaign's first sustained encounter with Magni, one of Thor's sons. Magni is rendered as a swaggering Aesir warrior whose arrogance underestimates Kratos's combat repertoire.

    The chapter's central event is the first Magni duel, a sustained combat sequence that introduces the campaign's hardest boss-tier enemy class. Magni's brother Modi intervenes during the fight, and the two Aesir gods escape together, vowing to return with reinforcements.

    The chapter ends with the Magic Chisel acquired, the path to Tyr's Temple's deeper chambers unlocked, and Magni and Modi positioned as the campaign's mid-game pursuit.

  7. 7

    7. Inside the Mountain

    The campaign's largest sustained dungeon. The Mountain — visible from every part of the Lake of Nine, the destination of Faye's funeral journey — is revealed to be the campaign's most architecturally ambitious environment. The chapter is structured as a multi-level vertical exploration, with the mountain's hollow interior containing a vast Aesir-built infrastructure of mining operations, dwarven outposts, and the Black Heart of the Mountain — a pollutant Odin has been using to poison the realm.

    The chapter dwells on the campaign's environmental storytelling. Audio logs left by surviving Jötnar, dwarven shop notes, and Mimir's expanded narration reveal the Aesir's centuries-long campaign to weaponize the mountain's resources. The chapter introduces several of the campaign's hardest combat encounters, including the Soul Eater boss confrontation in the mountain's central chamber.

    The chapter ends with the Black Heart purified, Atreus's growing combat capability now visibly outpacing his father's expectations, and the mountain's deepest chamber — containing critical lore about Faye — opened.

  8. 8

    8. The Sickness — Atreus Falls Ill

    The campaign's emotional pivot. Atreus, returning from the mountain, falls into a serious illness that Freya identifies as a god-sickness — a disease that occurs when a young deity refuses to accept their own nature. The chapter is structured as a sustained quiet sequence in Freya's sanctuary, with Atreus comatose and Kratos forced to confront the central reveal he has been avoiding: Atreus is half-god, and Kratos has been hiding this from him out of fear of what the boy might become.

    The chapter's pivot is the journey to Helheim — the Norse afterlife — to retrieve a heart of Bifröst that Freya needs for Atreus's cure. The chapter weaves the franchise's signature Greek-era references into the descent, with Kratos's Greek past explicitly surfacing for the first time in Norse-era combat sequences.

    The chapter ends with Kratos descending into Helheim alone, the campaign's most acclaimed perspective-pivot committed, and the Greek-era references that have been threaded throughout the campaign now openly acknowledged.

  9. 9

    9. The Black Rune — Helheim's Frozen Realm

    The campaign's third-act darkest chapter. Helheim is rendered as the franchise's most distinct alternate realm — a frozen wasteland of Greek-style underworld imagery overlaid on Norse architectural framework, with the resident dead spirits constantly chanting in Greek. The chapter is structured as a sustained survival-combat sequence with the Leviathan Axe's freeze abilities disabled.

    The chapter's central event is Kratos's confrontation with his own Greek past. Spirits from Greece — Spartan warriors, Olympic minions, Greek-era civilians — surface throughout the realm, and the chapter dwells on Kratos's slow recognition that his past cannot be hidden from Atreus forever. The chapter introduces the campaign's most thematically loaded combat sequences.

    The chapter ends with the Bifröst heart acquired, Atreus's cure secured, and Kratos returning to Midgard with the campaign's central thematic question — when will he tell Atreus the truth — fully active.

  10. 10

    10. The Mountain's Secrets — Magni and Modi

    The campaign's mid-third-act combat chapter. With Atreus recovered, Kratos and his son return to the mountain to access its highest summit. The chapter introduces the Modi-and-Magni reunion — the brothers, having spent the chapter offscreen rebuilding their forces, ambush Kratos and Atreus at the mountain's central ridge.

    The chapter's combat highlights include the campaign's first sustained Aesir-god duel sequence. Magni is killed by Kratos in the chapter's mid-section — a deliberate franchise-statement moment, the first time the new Kratos has explicitly killed a god in the Norse era. Modi escapes, broken and humiliated. The chapter's emotional weight lands on Atreus's response to the killing. The boy, fully recovered from his illness and now fully aware of his god-sickness diagnosis, has begun displaying the franchise's signature traits of god-tier arrogance.

    The chapter ends with Magni dead, Modi in flight, and Atreus's character pivot fully committed.

  11. 11

    11. Atreus Changes — The Boy Becomes a God

    The campaign's character-pivot chapter. Atreus, now openly aware that he is half-god, begins behaving with the casual cruelty the franchise has always associated with divine status. He kills wounded enemies. He talks back to Kratos with new contempt. He picks fights he cannot win.

    The chapter is structured as a sustained character-deterioration arc weaving combat with quiet domestic scenes. The chapter's pivot is the second Modi encounter. Modi, already broken from the prior chapter, is helpless before Kratos's blade — but Atreus kills him personally in an act of casual cruelty that Kratos cannot prevent. The chapter dwells on Kratos's horror at recognizing his Greek-era self in his son's behavior.

    The chapter ends with Modi dead by Atreus's hand, Kratos's quiet decision to finally tell Atreus the truth about their family heritage, and the campaign's emotional crescendo positioned for the final journey to Jötunheim.

  12. 12

    12. The Stranger Returns — Baldur

    The campaign's penultimate combat chapter. The Stranger — revealed across the chapter as Baldur, son of Odin and Freya — returns for his final confrontation with Kratos. The chapter dwells on the campaign's central character reveal: Baldur is Freya's son, cursed by his own mother to be invulnerable to all attacks (and thus also to all sensations). His pursuit of Kratos has been pursued on Odin's orders to prevent the journey to Jötunheim.

    The chapter's combat highlights include the campaign's most demanding sustained duel sequence. Baldur is faster, more aggressive, and more genuinely dangerous than any prior antagonist. The fight ranges across the Lake of Nine, into Tyr's Temple, and ends with Baldur's invulnerability finally broken — by an unintended act involving mistletoe, the mythological key to his curse.

    The chapter ends with Baldur defeated but Freya intervening to protect her son one final time, the campaign's mother-and-son tragedy fully exposed, and the final journey to Jötunheim committed.

  13. 13

    13. The Journey to Jötunheim

    The campaign's climactic chapter. Kratos and Atreus, having defeated Baldur and recovered Faye's ashes, ascend the Jötunheim tower for the final journey to the realm of the giants. The chapter is structured as a sustained quiet sequence — the actual combat is brief; the chapter's weight lands on the slow vertical ascent.

    The chapter's central reveal is staged inside Jötunheim itself. The realm's hidden temples contain murals depicting the entire campaign's events in advance — Faye's death, the journey, Baldur's pursuit, every major narrative beat. Faye was a Jötunn. She came to Midgard to find Kratos because Jötnar prophecy had identified him as the father of her son. Atreus's Jötunn name is Loki — the trickster god of Norse mythology, the future architect of Ragnarök.

    The chapter dwells on the murals' deeper implication: Faye chose this journey for Kratos and Atreus specifically because it would bring them together, force them to confront their shared identity, and ensure Atreus's full development into the god he is destined to become.

  14. 14

    14. Mother's Ashes — Epilogue

    The campaign's closing chapter. Kratos and Atreus reach the highest peak in the Nine Realms — Jötunheim's central summit — and scatter Faye's ashes together. The chapter is structured as a quiet sustained sequence with minimal dialogue and one of the franchise's most acclaimed musical cues.

    The chapter's final scene is staged back at the family cabin. The journey is complete. Kratos and Atreus return to a domestic life that has now been permanently reshaped by the campaign's events. Atreus, now fully aware of his Jötunn name and his god-tier heritage, asks Kratos about his Greek past. Kratos, after a long pause, agrees to tell him — but later. Not yet.

    The chapter's post-credits scene establishes the franchise's continuation. Thor, son of Odin, arrives at the cabin door in a thunderstorm — the explicit setup for Ragnarök. The credits roll over the franchise's signature score, with the campaign's central question — what kind of man Kratos has become in the Norse wilds — answered through the quiet domestic restoration of his role as father.
