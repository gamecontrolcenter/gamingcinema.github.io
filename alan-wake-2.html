About the Game

Alan Wake 2 is Remedy Entertainment's long-awaited sequel to the 2010 cult horror title, switching between FBI Agent Saga Anderson investigating a ritual murder in Bright Falls and novelist Alan Wake trapped in the Dark Place. The walkthrough captures the full game across two parts on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling, no commentary. Remedy's signature live-action FMV cutscenes intercut with the cinematic gameplay.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. Return — The Cauldron Lake Murder

    The campaign opens with FBI Special Agent Saga Anderson arriving in Bright Falls, Washington alongside her partner Alex Casey to investigate a ritualistic murder near Cauldron Lake. The opening hour is structured as a procedural police investigation — interview transcripts, crime-scene photography, autopsy reports — with the supernatural staying firmly off-camera until the chapter's mid-point. The Bright Falls of Alan Wake II is rendered as a town visibly worse off than the original game's: economically depressed, drug-touched, and quietly haunted by the lake at its center.

    The chapter's central reveal is the body itself. The victim is found ritualistically arranged with pages from an unwritten manuscript stitched into his torso, and the autopsy reveals that the victim's heart has been removed and replaced with a different one — the heart of a man missing for thirteen years. The man is Robert Nightingale, the FBI agent from the original Alan Wake who disappeared into Cauldron Lake at that game's conclusion. The case immediately resists rational explanation.

    The chapter introduces the campaign's Mind Place mechanic — Saga's psychological investigation space, where she pins photographs, interviews, and evidence to a cork-board and runs deductive logic in deliberate, slow-motion sequences. The Mind Place is the campaign's most acclaimed UI design and one of its most thematically loaded systems: Saga's mind is, in the campaign's own metaphysics, a location that other things can enter.

    The chapter ends with Saga and Casey checking into the Bright Falls motel for an investigation expected to last three days. It will not last three days.

  2. 2

    2. Late Night — The Dark Place

    The campaign's first Alan Wake chapter. Alan, missing for thirteen years since the conclusion of the original game, is revealed to be alive — barely — inside the Dark Place, an extradimensional prison made of nightmare-logic versions of New York City. The chapter opens with Alan emerging from black water in a subway station, with no memory of how long he has been there, and the Dark Place rendered as the campaign's most aesthetically distinctive environment: a Lynchian Manhattan analog where streetlights stutter, posters change between glances, and the city's architecture twists at impossible angles.

    The chapter's central mechanic introduction is the Plot Board — Alan's narrative-modification system, in which he writes scenes onto a story-corkboard and the Dark Place itself rewrites to accommodate the new draft. The mechanic is one of the most original puzzle systems in modern gaming: the player must rewrite the locations of items, the identities of NPCs, and the meanings of objects in order to navigate the Dark Place's geography.

    The chapter's atmospheric anchor is the Cult of the Word — a hooded faction of Dark Place residents who worship Alan as the prophet of their own escape. Their leader, the Mr. Door-affiliated figure who recurs throughout the campaign, is introduced through one of the most unsettling cult-recruitment sequences in modern horror gaming. The chapter ends with Alan accepting that he is going to have to write his way out, and that the writing itself is going to summon worse things into Bright Falls.

  3. 3

    3. Local Girl — The Watery Investigation

    Saga's investigation extends north to Watery, a sub-region of the Bright Falls jurisdiction where the murdered man — Robert Nightingale — was last seen alive thirteen years ago. The chapter is structured as a slow-burn investigation across the lakeside town, with witness interviews, evidence collection, and one of the campaign's most uncomfortable interrogation sequences against a possessed local resident.

    The chapter's central thread is the introduction of the Anderson Brothers — Tor and Odin Anderson, the aged frontmen of the fictional rock band Old Gods of Asgard whose music has been threaded through the franchise since the original game. The brothers are now elderly, living in a sprawling rural farm property, and visibly tangled with the supernatural in ways even they don't fully understand. Their farmhouse becomes one of the campaign's recurring locations.

    The chapter ends with Saga uncovering a connection between Nightingale's murder, the Anderson Brothers' old recordings, and a manuscript fragment that appears to predict the murders before they happen. The supernatural is no longer deniable. The case is no longer procedural.

  4. 4

    4. Initiation — The Cult of the Word

    Alan's second sustained chapter inside the Dark Place. The chapter is structured as a slow-creeping infiltration of the Cult of the Word's central hideout — a Lynchian, fluorescent-lit warehouse complex where the cult's members chant the manuscripts Alan has involuntarily written across the decade. The cult's worship is rendered with the kind of skin-crawling specificity that places this chapter among the most acclaimed horror sequences in modern gaming.

    The chapter's central reveal is Scratch — Alan's doppelganger, an entity that has been writing alongside him from the Dark Place and is now manifesting in the real world. Scratch is presented as Alan's shadow self, a violent, predatory version of the man Alan was before he stopped writing thrillers. The campaign frames the Scratch threat as functionally an evil twin: indistinguishable from Alan in photographs, indistinguishable from Alan to his own wife, and committed to destroying Alan's path home.

    The chapter's gameplay loop deepens the Plot Board mechanic with sustained narrative-rewrite puzzles, including the campaign's first sustained chase sequence against the Dark Place's primary predators. The chapter ends with Alan committing to a manuscript that will free him — and the realization that completing it will require Saga's cooperation across the dimensional barrier.

  5. 5

    5. The Heart — Saga's Mind Place Crisis

    The campaign's mid-game turning point. Saga's investigation has uncovered enough evidence to enter a Mind Place vision of the Cauldron Lake murder itself, and the chapter is structured as a sustained sequence inside Saga's own deductive imagination. The Mind Place opens out into a multi-room interior — case files, family photographs, childhood memories — and the chapter dwells on Saga's history in ways the procedural opening hours refused to.

    The chapter's central reveal is that Saga has been manipulated by the Dark Place from the beginning of the case. Her invitation to Bright Falls, her partnership with Casey, and her family's connection to the Anderson Brothers are all elements of a story Alan has been writing from inside the Dark Place — sometimes consciously, sometimes not. The chapter recontextualizes the entire campaign as a question of authorship: who is writing whom, and whether anyone in the Bright Falls case has actual agency.

    The chapter ends with Saga's discovery of a hidden Mind Place chamber containing photographs of her own daughter — who in some versions of the story is dead, in others alive, in others never existed. The metaphysics of the Dark Place have begun to bleed into the real world.

  6. 6

    6. Old Gods of Asgard — Herald of Darkness

    The campaign's most acclaimed single sequence and one of the most ambitious set-pieces in modern gaming. Saga's investigation leads her to the Old Gods of Asgard concert hall, a derelict Coca-Cola Hall venue beneath the Anderson Brothers' farm, where the band's classic-rock catalog has been weaponized into a literal reality-rewriting ritual. The chapter is structured as a single sustained performance of the song Herald of Darkness, with the Anderson Brothers re-recording themselves onstage in real-time while the chapter's combat and platforming sequences unfold in front of the audience.

    The chapter is half music video, half rhythm-action set-piece, half combat encounter. The Anderson Brothers narrate Alan's entire backstory in song while the player navigates the venue's stage, lighting rigs, and audience pit against Taken-class enemies. The sequence has been cited as one of the most distinctive in PlayStation gaming history.

    The chapter ends with Saga learning the full scope of the campaign's antagonist — not Scratch, not the Cult, but the entity that has been using Alan's writing as a vector for entering the real world. The chapter's title track plays out over a slow camera pull through the now-empty concert hall.

  7. 7

    7. Zane's Film — The Live-Action Hour

    A chapter that briefly reshapes the entire campaign's medium. Alan, deep in the Dark Place's narrative-rewrite territory, accesses a sustained sequence inside Thomas Zane's film archives — Zane being the metafictional director from the original Alan Wake who is now revealed to be a central figure in the Remedy Connected Universe's mythology. The chapter intercuts gameplay with roughly an hour of live-action film footage featuring real actors, real sets, and a sustained narrative segment that plays as a self-contained short film.

    The chapter is the campaign's most experimental and one of its most divisive sequences. The live-action footage stages Zane's perspective on the events of the Dark Place, the role of the Mr. Door-affiliated antagonist, and the broader Remedy multiverse including Control's Federal Bureau of Control. The chapter functions as both a deep-lore download and a tonal disruption that the campaign's final third pays off.

    The chapter ends with Alan in possession of the film's central macguffin — a manuscript fragment that, when combined with Saga's evidence in the real world, will collapse the dimensional barrier and force the campaign's final confrontation.

  8. 8

    8. Deerfest — The Bright Falls Festival

    Saga's chapter dedicated to the Deerfest, Bright Falls's annual harvest festival, which the campaign has been pacing toward since the opening hour. The chapter is staged across the festival grounds at dusk and into night, with carnival rides, drunken locals, and a slow-creeping sense that everyone in attendance is either possessed, hallucinating, or actively being rewritten by the Dark Place's encroachment.

    The chapter's central event is the possession of half the festival audience by Taken-class entities, with Saga forced to navigate combat sequences against people she has interviewed throughout the case. The chapter's emotional weight comes from the impossibility of saving most of them — the campaign refuses heroic restoration, and the festival ends with significant casualties.

    The chapter ends with Saga finally locating the Cauldron Lake entry point that Alan has been navigating from the other side, and the campaign's two protagonists confirmed to be on the verge of meeting for the first time in thirteen years.

  9. 9

    9. Come Home — The Final Manuscript

    The campaign's climactic chapter. Alan and Saga, working in coordinated rewrites across the dimensional barrier, attempt to collapse the Dark Place's grip on Bright Falls before Scratch can complete his own takeover. The chapter is structured as a parallel-action finale, with Alan navigating the Dark Place's deepest layers and Saga defending the Cauldron Lake breach in the real world.

    The chapter's mid-section is the campaign's most acclaimed boss confrontation: a multi-stage duel against Scratch that demands mastery of every system both characters have taught — Alan's narrative rewrites, Saga's case-board deductions, the campaign's combat fundamentals, and the Plot Board's environmental manipulation. The fight is staged across both dimensions simultaneously, with cuts between Alan and Saga's perspectives happening in mid-combat.

    The chapter closes with the manuscript completed, Scratch defeated, and the campaign's final cliffhanger: Alan does not return to the real world. The barrier closes with him still inside the Dark Place, having sacrificed his own escape to ensure Saga's. The campaign's epilogue — staged in a half-lit hospital room — sets up The Lake House and Night Springs expansions while leaving the franchise's central question unresolved. The cycle is not yet over.
