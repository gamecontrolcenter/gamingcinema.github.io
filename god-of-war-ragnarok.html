About the Game

God of War Ragnarok concludes Santa Monica Studio's Norse saga, following Kratos and his son Atreus through the prophesied end of the Aesir gods. The walkthrough captures the full main campaign across two parts on PS5 Pro at 4K HDR 60FPS with PSSR upscaling. Combat layers the Leviathan Axe, Blades of Chaos, and the new Draupnir Spear with refined parries and runic attacks. All nine realms are explored in cinematic fidelity, no commentary.

Walkthrough — Chapter Breakdown

A complete chapter-by-chapter overview of the campaign. Use this guide to track story progress, locate specific moments in the videos above, or plan a multi-session viewing.

  1. 1

    1. Surviving Fimbulwinter

    The opening chapter picks up three years after Baldur's death at the close of God of War (2018). The Aesir's promised winter — Fimbulwinter — has frozen Midgard solid, and Kratos and Atreus have spent the intervening years training, hunting, and trying to disappear from a world that knows what they have done. Atreus has visibly aged, more curious about his Jötunn heritage and the name Loki he saw on the wall in Jötunheim. Kratos remains guarded, monosyllabic, and increasingly aware that hiding will not work forever.

    The chapter's central encounter is the arrival of Thor Odinson at the cabin door. The confrontation is staged in the snowfall around the cabin and across the mountain face beyond it, a multi-phase brawl that becomes one of the most acclaimed opening duels in modern action gaming. Thor is staged as a genuinely terrifying physical presence — armored, drunk, full of grief over his fallen sons, and capable of taking everything Kratos throws at him without flinching. Mjölnir's lightning, the explosive throws, the wrestling clinches, and the eventual stalemate all serve to reset the franchise's power scale: Kratos can survive a god of Thor's caliber, but he cannot defeat one in a clean fight.

    The chapter closes with Odin arriving on Sleipnir to mediate. The Allfather's proposal of peace is delivered with the easy menace of a man who has already won most of the negotiation. Kratos refuses, Odin departs, and the chapter ends with Kratos and Atreus deciding — over Mimir's objections — to seek out the lost god Týr for help against the inevitable war.

  2. 2

    2. The Quest for Týr

    The chapter opens with the journey to Svartalfheim, the dwarven realm of the Nine, accessed through a new Yggdrasil branch unlocked by Mimir. Svartalfneim is one of the campaign's standout aesthetic achievements — a steampunk industrial mining culture built into vast subterranean caverns, lit by molten metal and the bioluminescent algae of the underground lakes. The dwarves of Nidavellir are caught between Aesir mining contracts and their own dying sovereignty, and the realm is suffering for it.

    The mid-chapter sequence introduces the Lyngbakr mini-quest, in which Kratos and Atreus liberate a giant whale-creature chained to the seabed by an experimental Aesir contraption. The Lyngbakr quest also introduces the realm's open-zone exploration loop, the Sigrun temple favors, and the dwarven smithing infrastructure that Brok and Sindri will eventually re-anchor in the story. A confrontation with the Dreki mini-boss serves as the chapter's combat exam.

    The chapter ends with Atreus and Kratos finally locating Týr in a hidden Aesir-built prison. The reunion is tense and immediate — Týr is bearded, broken, and clearly traumatized by Odin's torture. He resists Atreus's enthusiasm for war, preaches non-violence, and warns of the cost of fulfilling the prophecy. The chapter closes with Týr agreeing to travel with them, a fragile alliance that will define the next half of the campaign.

  3. 3

    3. The Reckoning

    This is the chapter that pivots the campaign onto its second axis. Atreus, increasingly convinced that he can resolve the war through diplomacy, secretly accepts Odin's invitation to visit Asgard. He passes between realms using the Bifröst-mask disguise Sindri has been quietly making for him and arrives in the Aesir capital under the protection of Thor's daughter Thrúd.

    Asgard itself is rendered as a gleaming, sun-soaked city — visibly more beautiful and culturally rich than the dark forests of Midgard or the industrial Svartalfheim. The contrast is intentional and deeply uncomfortable. The chapter dwells on the Aesir not as cartoon villains but as people: Thrúd's training routines, Sif's domestic conflicts with Thor's drinking, the petty politics of Odin's inner circle. Atreus is genuinely charmed.

    The central sequence is Atreus's audience with Odin himself. The Allfather presents as soft-spoken, intellectually curious, and apparently sincere in his desire to avert Ragnarök through cooperation. He shows Atreus a fragment of the broken mask that supposedly opens a rift to a forgotten realm, and asks for Atreus's help in completing it. The chapter ends with Atreus accepting — a decision he will hide from Kratos through most of the second act.

  4. 4

    4. Old Friends

    A character-focused chapter set largely at Sindri's house, the dwarven brothers' pocket realm hidden between branches of Yggdrasil. The chapter has comparatively little combat; instead, it works as a tonal reset and a foundation for the relationships that will be tested by the final third of the game. Mimir reconnects with Sif's father, Sindri and Brok argue about the safety of their guests, and Týr quietly observes the family Kratos has built.

    The chapter's emotional centerpiece is a quiet conversation between Kratos and Atreus about Faye — Atreus's mother — and what she would have wanted from this moment. Kratos's slow opening up across the trilogy reaches one of its most vulnerable notes here. The scene is staged in dim light, on the porch, with Mimir's head as the only witness.

    A second arc inside the chapter involves Sindri's growing unease with his brother Brok, who he believes is hiding something serious. The thread is left dangling — but it will detonate later. The chapter closes with Týr revealing a plan to access the realm between realms through the Norns at the trunk of Yggdrasil, setting up the next major story beat.

  5. 5

    5. Groa's Secret

    The chapter takes Kratos and Mimir to Vanaheim, the verdant realm of the Vanir, in pursuit of a path to the spirit of the giantess Groa. The journey forces Kratos to accept the partnership of Freya, who is still actively trying to kill him for her son Baldur's death. The opening hours of the chapter weave their reluctant truce around the realm's invasive jungle ecology — Vanaheim is rendered as the franchise's most visually lush environment, full of waterfalls, ancient ruins, and Mongol-tiger-sized predators.

    The chapter's central reveal is that Freya has been cursed by Odin to remain inside Vanaheim, unable to leave or use the powerful Vanir magic that was her birthright. Removing the curse requires retrieving a piece of her soul kept by the Aesir, a quest that takes Kratos through some of the campaign's best-designed dungeons. Along the way, Freya begins — slowly, reluctantly — to forgive Kratos. The arc is one of the most emotionally complete in the entire franchise.

    The chapter closes with Freya's curse broken, her old powers as Vanadís restored, and her brother Frey's long story rejoining the campaign. The newly empowered Freya commits to the war effort, opening Vanaheim's hub area to side content and introducing The Crater, the campaign's largest single optional zone.

  6. 6

    6. The Word of Fate

    A ritual journey through Yggdrasil's roots to meet the three Norns — the Aesir's seers of fate. The Norns sequence is one of the campaign's most thematically dense scenes, staged inside a candle-lit chamber at the base of the world tree where the women weave possibility into prophecy. Their dialogue with Kratos, Atreus, and Týr is deliberately unsettling: every prophecy they describe sounds inevitable, until they reveal that prophecy itself is just the most-walked path of every previous person who believed it.

    The conversation re-centers the campaign's question — not can Kratos avoid his fate, but whether the very idea of fate is a self-fulfilling story Odin has weaponized. The Norns also reveal that Týr is not who he appears to be, planting the first proper foreshadowing of the campaign's biggest twist.

    The chapter's gameplay loop weaves between two parallel tracks: Kratos searching the deep roots and Atreus simultaneously infiltrating Asgard's library for forbidden knowledge. The transition between the two is handled cleanly, and the chapter ends with both threads converging on a single decision: they must take the war to Asgard directly.

  7. 7

    7. Forging Destiny

    The campaign's most action-dense story chapter. Kratos and Atreus, now committed to opening Ragnarök on their own terms, set out to retrieve the Spear of Faye — a weapon hidden in Asgard that Faye left for Kratos as her final gift. The retrieval requires breaking into Asgard at night, and the resulting infiltration is one of the franchise's most cinematic level designs.

    The chapter's central encounter is the duel with Heimdall, watchman of the Bifröst Bridge. Heimdall is the campaign's most arrogant and the most genuinely difficult opponent — his precognitive ability deflects normal attacks, forcing Kratos to land hits through the Spear's unique aerial properties. The fight is staged across multiple arenas, ends with one of the franchise's most controversial finisher animations, and produces the Spear of Faye as Kratos's permanent third weapon.

    The chapter's gut-punch is its closing scene. After the duel, Brok is killed by a possessed Odin-puppet — Týr is revealed in the next chapter to be Odin in disguise, but the murder happens in Forging Destiny. Brok's death is staged quietly, almost domestically, in Sindri's kitchen, and it lands as one of the most devastating moments in the franchise. Sindri's wordless grief at the end of the chapter sets up the campaign's quietest tragedy.

  8. 8

    8. Reunion

    A relatively short story chapter framed around Atreus's return from his prolonged Asgard mission. The opening minutes are dedicated to Sindri's funeral arrangements for Brok, with the surviving cast gathered around the body — Kratos, Atreus, Freya, Mimir, Sif, and a now-revealed-as-fraudulent Týr. The reveal that the Týr who has been traveling with them is Odin in disguise lands here in full, and the chapter pivots into a brief but vicious confrontation that nearly tears the entire alliance apart.

    The mid-chapter sequence introduces the assault on Asgard as the campaign's now-certain conclusion. Kratos and Atreus reconcile their separate plans, the giants of the lost Jötunn race are brought back into play through Angrboda's contact network, and the surviving Aesir defectors are organized into the resistance army that will march on the capital.

    The chapter ends with the campaign's central question fully reframed: the world will end, but will Kratos's son end with it? This is the last quiet chapter before the war.

  9. 9

    9. The Realms at War

    A connective chapter that moves the campaign's combatants into position across the Nine Realms. Kratos travels to gather the Aesir's enemies — the giants, the Vanir, the surviving dwarves, the Hel-walkers — while Atreus is dispatched to Helheim alone to recover a critical ally. The chapter alternates perspectives between the two of them, the first time in the franchise that Atreus is sustained as a playable lead for an extended sequence.

    The chapter's mid-section delivers one of the campaign's most memorable side encounters: the duel with Garm, the giant wolf imprisoned beneath Helheim. Garm's release is required for Atreus to access the deeper sections of the realm, but the wolf's transformation in death — into something else, something more important — sets up the campaign's third-act revelation.

    The chapter closes with Kratos's forces converging at the Wildwoods and Atreus reuniting with Angrboda in Jötunheim, where the campaign's most thematically loaded scenes — about lineage, about what is owed to ancestors, about whether Atreus is or is not Loki — are finally allowed to breathe.

  10. 10

    10. Unleashing Hel

    Atreus's most sustained playable chapter. The young god travels deeper into Helheim with Angrboda and a reborn-as-pup Fenrir — the second half of Garm's spiritual transformation. The chapter dwells on Atreus's quiet acceptance of his Loki name and the heritage that comes with it: a god of mischief, of stories, of changing what people believe is possible.

    The chapter is full of small character beats. Angrboda's family history with the Jötnar, her conversations with Atreus about whether the giants want vengeance or peace, and the journey through the cold gray ruins of Hel itself all combine to produce one of the campaign's most thematically satisfying middle sections.

    The chapter's combat highlights include the Helheim wolves, a confrontation with possessed Hel-walkers, and a quiet boss encounter with one of Odin's spies. The chapter ends with Atreus returning to the main campaign carrying Fenrir's reborn form and a clearer sense of what Loki means in the prophecy he has been trying to reshape.

  11. 11

    11. Creatures of Prophecy

    A spectacle-heavy chapter that escalates the war's mythological scale. The chapter centers on the awakening of Surtr, the fire giant prophesied to burn Asgard at Ragnarök, and on Kratos's preparation of the giant's army of allies. Surtr's section is staged in Muspelheim, a furnace-and-stone realm of red light and lava rivers, and his slow transformation into the ultimate weapon of the prophecy serves as one of the campaign's most cinematic set-pieces.

    The chapter also folds in Sól and Máni, the wolf-pursued sun and moon of Norse cosmology, in a brief but visually arresting sequence that opens the path to the campaign's final act.

    The chapter closes with Surtr's transformation into Sutr-the-end-of-all-things complete, and the alliance fully positioned for the assault on Asgard.

  12. 12

    12. The Summoning

    A largely combat-free chapter dedicated to assembling the army for the final battle. The chapter visits each of the major allied factions one last time — the Vanir, the Hel-walkers, the Jötnar, the dwarven refugees, the surviving Aesir defectors — and lets each have one final scene with Kratos and Atreus before the war begins.

    The chapter's most quietly powerful sequence is Sindri's refusal to participate. Still consumed by grief over Brok, he locks Kratos out of his home and explicitly tells the protagonist that he is no longer welcome. The scene is staged without music. Kratos accepts it without argument. The chapter does not resolve the Sindri thread — it simply leaves the wound open for the campaign's finale to reopen.

    The chapter ends with the army poised at the edge of the Bifröst, ready to begin.

  13. 13

    13. Ragnarök

    The campaign's title chapter and its climactic action sequence. The assault on Asgard begins with a coordinated multi-front invasion: Surtr's fire-giant form burns through the city walls from the outside, while Kratos, Atreus, Freya, and the surviving allies push toward Odin's throne room from within. The chapter is structured as a series of escalating set-piece battles, each handing off to the next with cinematic intensity.

    The chapter's combat highlights include the second duel with Thor — staged as a deliberate emotional inversion of the opening chapter's brawl, with Thor now genuinely uncertain about his father's cause — and the assault on Odin's chamber itself. Thor's eventual death at Odin's hand reframes the Allfather as the campaign's only true antagonist, and pushes Kratos's confrontation with him into pure mythological scale.

    The boss fight with Odin is the campaign's mechanical apex, demanding mastery of every weapon, every shield archetype, every Spartan Rage mode, and every runic attack the player has unlocked. The fight is structured in three phases that pull Odin through his many faces: scholar, manipulator, and finally a desperate, godhood-stripped old man.

    The chapter ends with Odin defeated but not yet dead — and Sindri returning to claim a private vengeance for his brother's death. The closing image of Sindri smashing Odin's soul-mask is one of the franchise's most haunting.

  14. 14

    14. Cycle of Vengeance

    The campaign's quiet epilogue. Asgard has fallen. The prophecy has been fulfilled, but on Kratos and Atreus's terms — and at costs none of them anticipated. The chapter is set in the days after the battle, mostly in the ruins of Sindri's home and the wider Nine Realms, with the surviving cast slowly accepting that the war is genuinely over.

    The chapter's emotional payoff is Atreus's farewell. The young god, now visibly more confident in his Loki identity, leaves Kratos to seek out the remaining scattered Jötnar across the realms. The departure is staged at dawn, on a Vanaheim mountainside, with Kratos finally allowed to be a father rather than a god of war. The final scene — Faye's mural of their journey rendered in the Jötunheim style — has become one of PlayStation's defining images.

    The chapter ends with Kratos taking up his role as a quietly reformed leader in the rebuilt realms, with the door explicitly left open for future stories.
